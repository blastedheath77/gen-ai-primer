Hallucination: Confident but Wrong

When prediction goes off the rails

An LLM doesn't "know" things — it predicts likely-sounding continuations. It can generate text that is fluent, confident, and completely false: citing papers that don't exist, stating incorrect facts, fabricating plausible details. This isn't a bug to be patched — it's an inherent consequence of the prediction mechanism.

Key Points

  • Hallucination is structural, not a bug — the model optimises for likelihood, not truth
  • Confidence in tone has zero correlation with accuracy
  • This is why human verification remains essential for any factual AI output
  • The model predicts what sounds right, not what is right

If models can hallucinate and generate harmful content, how do companies make them safer? The answer involves something surprising: human feedback.
